LEADERSHIP PHILOSOPHY (MY 9 RULES)

YOU DON'T HAVE TO BE A MANAGER TO BE A LEADER

As I began to focus more on creating leaders out of individual contributors, I learned the biggest misconception for others was the idea that they too needed direct reports. There’s an individual I worked with a few roles ago who desperately wanted to be a manager, but eventually realized that as a leader there was much more flexibility in rallying individuals behind his initiative. By having managers as sponsors, he was able to create efforts that elevate himself first and others along for the ride, as opposed to elevating others first and foremost in a management role. He would bring similar job functions together through all these grass roots efforts, maneuvering between cross-functional teams to move the needle in his direction. He became a master of the art of persuasion, and everything this individual thought he loved about management, turned out to be the very things he really loved about leadership without the responsibility and overhead.

NOTHING SHOULD TAKE LONGER THAN HIGH SCHOOL

A mentor of mine starts these business ventures at what feels like every other month. When I asked how he knew when it was time to close the book on one project and start something new, his response was, “keep a consistent process with a defined timeline.” It takes a year and half to bring an idea to market, another year and half to improve and scale it, and then a year to give it to someone else to run. If our goals are designed to improve ourselves and our situation, whether it be starting a new company, completing a screenplay, or recording an album, nothing we start should take more than 4 years.

FAIL FIRST, AND FAIL FAST

A buddy of mine has been perfecting his magna opus for 7 years and no one has ever read it. Moving forward and attaining a successful outcome requires putting yourself and your work out there, to let it be torn apart. Otherwise nothing is ever actually awesome, or awful. We get in our own way and the same applies for the excuses we make to NOT fail first, not fail fast at our jobs and the side hustles. Whatever it is, get it done as soon as possible, get it in front of others and relish in the moment anyone tells you that “it needs work.”

ALWAYS TAKE A JOB YOU CAN 'BARELY', JUST BARELY DO

I constantly meet talent under 35 that have somehow managed to be in the same job for 10+ years. And even more strange, these individuals have no intentions of ever leaving their company or switching roles. Job Security is everything to them. You’re wasting time applying to a role where you easily check off all the requirements of the application. Go after the roles where you meet the minimum requirements and barely meet any of the desired ones. Even better, apply for the position you're passionate about and meet 30% of the minimum requirements. You’ll be surprised when you don’t short change yourself.

WORK WITH PEOPLE YOU WOULD NEVER HANG OUT WITH

There’s an expression out there, “Great talent knows great talent.” This may be true, but in my experience great talent doesn't refer great talent, they usually refer their friends. The problem with this isn’t that your friends suck; it’s that your ideas become stale. You’re too comfortable around them, and eventually the lines just blur. I once asked my manager on his last day of the job if he had any advice for me. He said, “I hired you because I knew we’d never have the same idea and we’d rarely agree on the same thing, and I was right. Don’t hire someone like you, hire someone like me.”

STAY RELEVANT

You can become irrelevant without even knowing it, and for managers, staying relevant becomes an essential part of the job. For many, the management ladder is a means to explain why it’s okay to be disconnected. The age old tale that they now manage managers, they’re conscious of micromanaging, or even more ridiculous, that they’re supposed to be “operating at 30,000 feet”. Staying relevant not only requires you to know what your team does, but how they’re doing it without actually being in the weeds. If the day comes that you have no idea how long a task should take and can’t read the tea leaves when it comes to roadblocks and gaps in your team’s initiatives, then you’ve entered your stone age.

STRIVE FOR PERFECTION, SETTLE FOR GREATNESS

The hardest job I ever had was when I was a lead engineer at an ad agency, what feels like yesterday and a lifetime ago at the same time. This place beat me down in so many different ways. Everything I did was never good enough, with every deliverable requiring a frustrating amount of revisions. Even when I knew in my heart of hearts it was finally great, it got sent back for more tweaks. After I left this agency, it somehow made every job afterward feel more straightforward. When others struggled at their jobs, I was being praised for what would’ve been considered shit at the previous agency. They demanded perfection only so they could squeeze out great from me and somehow, it’s the only place I’d ever work for again.

DO NOT OVERINDEX ON NETWORKING

Networking isn’t a job, it’s not even a small part of the job, it’s the outcome of collaboration. Yet, some people force it and don’t realize they’ve made it an artificial experience for the other party. A buddy of mine has invested in networking so much he overleveraged himself to the point where people don’t want to deal with him. It's become clear he’s lost confidence in delivering anything tangible against his craft, and what he has today that he greatly values and believes is his most critical tool in his bag of offerings, is networking. Now it’s a survival game. In my experience, those that network aggressively are hiding behind the salt that they have no idea how to get recognized for their work or even worse, they don’t actually do any work. They talk about it, rally others, and are the bullhorn for 3rd degree accolades and achievements.

KNOW WHO YOU YOU WANT TO BE TOMORROW

From time to time, individuals ask me if they should consider management and my answer is always yes. The world needs shitty, good, and great managers. After all, being shitty is easy, being good requires work, but being great requires dedication to growth. If you hate presentations, there's a high probability you’ll never be great at management, but no one should tell you what to or not to pursue. They can only and should only point out the development area you need to work on to get to where you want to be. Who you are, what you’ll become, and how you evolve from the now to the tomorrow is achievable as long as you know what you’re getting yourself into. Take all the risks you can, but never set yourself up for failure.
